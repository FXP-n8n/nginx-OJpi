The four things Brussels institutions actually automate

July 2026

“AI automation” is a broad enough phrase to mean almost anything, which makes it easy for a first conversation to wander. In practice, across associations, federations, policy media and EU-funded projects, the actual workload almost always falls into one of four families. It’s worth naming them, because the right starting point depends on which one is eating your team’s time.

1. Monitoring and triage. The most common by far: hundreds of source websites, newsletters, or consultation portals to watch, with most of what comes back being noise. This is the anchor use case — a harvesting-plus-triage pipeline that screens everything against your own editorial rubric and hands your team a ranked, explained shortlist instead of a firehose.

2. Conversational assistants. Once monitored content is structured and in one place, a natural next step is a chat layer on top of it — so staff can ask questions of the data instead of searching manually. We’ve shipped this as a production chatbot integrated directly into an existing dashboard, so it’s one more capability in a tool people already use, not a new one to learn.

3. Text analytics. Scoring or classifying written content at scale — for example, a computational tool that rates news articles on a war/peace-journalism spectrum, multilingual across French, Dutch and English. Useful anywhere an organisation needs a consistent, repeatable read on large volumes of text rather than one analyst’s manual judgement.

4. Document automation. Generating brand-conform documents from structured input — the same “encode the judgement, keep a human in review” pattern applied to output instead of input. We’ve built this for a travel agency generating client quotations and itineraries; the same approach applies to position papers, member reports, or dissemination deliverables.

Different institutions arrive at different families first, but the discipline underneath is the same across all four: deterministic logic wherever possible, AI where judgement is genuinely needed, and a human in the loop wherever the stakes are real.
